Part I: Introduction

Scattering length density (SLD) is a measure of a material's ability to scatter. SLD varies greatly depending on whether X-rays (SAXS) or neutrons (SANS) are used. This is because X-rays scatter from the electrons of a material, while neutrons scatter from the nuclei. In general, the SLD of a material can be calculated as: \begin{equation} \text{SLD} = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^n b_i}{V}, \end{equation} Where $b_i$ is the (bound coherent) scattering length of atom $i$, and $V$ is the molecular volume of the material. For X-rays, the scattering length of an atom is roughly proportional to the atomic number $Z$ of that atom. However for neutrons, the scattering lengths of atoms vary irregularly throughout the periodic table. This is illustrated in the figure below.


Comparison of coherent scattering lengths for Neutrons (red) and X-rays (Blue).

For neutrons, it is common to list the scattering length of an atom in units of fm (femtometers, $10^{-15}$ m). For our applications, the X-ray scattering length of an atom is essentially given by $b=Zr_e$, where $r_e = 2.82 \text{ fm}$ is the classical electron radius. This approximation holds because SAXS uses hard X-rays ($E>5-10 \text{ keV}$) with wavelengths dissimilar from those often absorbed by atoms in soft matter.

In practice, one should use a SLD Calculator. In Sasview, the SLD Calculator can be found under "Tools". Alternatively one can use an online calculator from NIST.

Most SAS experiments are performed in a solvent, which has a SLD of its own. If the SLD of the sample is similar to the solvent's SLD, the scattering from the sample will be weak. We measure this difference as excess scattering length density, \begin{equation} \Delta \text{SLD} = \text{SLD}_{\text{sample}} - \text{SLD}_{\text{solvent}}. \end{equation} The intensity of scattering from the sample is proportional to the $\Delta \text{SLD}^2$ which we call contrast.

Sometimes however, we want to match the SLD of the sample to the solvent, to make the sample or a part of the sample invisible. This technique is called contrast variation. In the exercise below, you will learn how to match out a protein.

    Exercise: Match out a protein in SANS

  • Assume that the SLD of a protein is $1.3 \times 10^{-6} \text{ Å}^{-2}$.
  • Calculate the percentages of H$_2$O and D$_2$O in your solvent in order to match out the protein ($\Delta \text{SLD}=0$). You can use the numbers you calculated in the previous exercise
  • Display hint

    To do this, you have to isolate x in the following equation \begin{equation} \Delta \text{SLD} =\text{SLD}_{\text{protein}} - x \times \text{SLD}_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} - (1-x) \times \text{SLD}_{\text{D}_2\text{O}} = 0, \end{equation} where $x$ is the percentage of H$_2$O in the solvent.

Part II: Sodium dodecyl sulfate

This section focuses on the detergent sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), CH$_3$(CH)$_2$OSO$_3$Na. SDS is widely used in SAS experiments for its ability to solubilize proteins effectively. As a first exercise, calculate the SLD of SDS for both X-rays and neutrons with the online calculator from NIST. For this you only need the molecular formula of SDS and the density which can be found on wikipedia. For the X-ray source assume an energy of 10 keV. You should see a table like the one below.

In reality, the SLD of SDS varies between the head group and the tail group. In addition to this, SDS forms micelles in solution with aggregation number $N=66$. In the exercise below, we calculate the forward scattering ($I(q=0)$) of SDS micelles, which is given by \begin{equation} I(q=0) = n N^2 \left( \Delta SLD_{tail} V_{tail} + \Delta SLD_{head} V_{head}\right)^2, \end{equation} where $n$ is the number density of the micelles.

Part III: Designing a neutron scattering experiment

In this part, we will design a SANS experiment to study the size and polydispersity of SiO$_2$ nanoparticles. Additionally, a cationic surfactant (CetylTrimethylAmmonium Bromide - CTAB) is added to the solution in a concentration close to the critical micelle concentration (CMC). The added CTAB is expected to form micelles around the nanoparticles with a thickness 2-3 nm. The added CTAB prohibits aggregation of SiO$_2$ nanoparticles. For our experiment we wish to gain information on both the SiO$_2$ nanoparticles and the CTAB coating. First we would like to find all the relevant SLDs.

Before we make a decision on what kind of experiment(s?) to perform, we should also consider incoherent scattering. Incoherent scattering contributes $|Q|$-independent background to the scattering. This makes it difficult to see features in the data, especially at low $Q$-values, where the signal is weaker. However, this is essentially only a problem for neutrons. Go back to the SLD calculator, and find the incoherent scattering cross section for H$_2$O and D$_2$O for neutrons.

    Task 3: Decide on experiment
  • Given everything we now know from task 1 and 2 which experiments should we perform? Display answer
    1. X-ray scattering with water as solvent. From the table in task 2, we see that the $\Delta \text{SLD}^2$ for silica is much higher than that of CTAB. It is even better in heavy water, but this is such a small difference that it is not worth the cost of the D$_2$O.
    2. Neutron scattering with heavy water as solvent. The $\Delta \text{SLD}^2$ for silica is ~$5.2$ times greater than that of CTAB. This isn't a huge difference, but we don't have to worry about the incoherent scattering from water. Additionally, we could match out the silica by adding some amount of H$_2$O to the solvent.
